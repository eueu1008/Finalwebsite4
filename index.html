Campbell's Soup I (1968)
Material: screen print
Dimensions: 50.8 x 40.6 cm
Locations: Multiple museums,
galleries, and collections
By the 1960s, even popular artworks of the Abstract Expressionists of the 1940s and
'50s had become cliche. Warhol was one of the artists that felt the need to bring
back imagery into his work. The gallery owner and interior designer Muriel Latow
gave Warhol the idea of painting soup cans, and suggested him to paint objects that
people use every day. Â Warhol then began to recreate images found in advertising.
He recreated images of products found in supermarkets in his canvas. Warhol is
highly respected to this day for creating a new type of art that both complemented
and criticized the consumption habits. Andy Warhols Campbells Soup Cans series
are most well-known images of American modern art. Warhol created as a series of
thirty two canvases and the soup cans gained international fame. Though they
seemed identical to the products in real life, slight variations appeared in the
lettering and in the hand-stamped fluer-de-lis symbols on the bottom of each soup
can.
